Conversational Artificial Intelligence

A 15-Week Master's Course on its History, Application, and Future

From Dialogue to Digital Minds

This course charts the evolution of conversational AI, from its philosophical origins in the Socratic method to the sophisticated Large Language Models (LLMs) of today. We will explore the foundational theories, pivotal technologies, and emerging architectures that enable machines to understand, reason, and converse. Students will gain a deep, technical understanding of systems like modern AI agents and their orchestration, preparing them for the next frontier of human-computer interaction.

A Timeline of Thought and Technology

1

Ancient Dialogues

The works of Plato and Galileo Galilei establish dialogue as a fundamental tool for learning and discovery, a concept that underpins modern conversational AI.

2

1950-1966: Foundations

Alan Turing proposes the "Imitation Game" to test machine intelligence. Claude Shannon's work on information theory lays mathematical groundwork. Joseph Weizenbaum creates ELIZA, an early chatbot that mimics a psychotherapist.

3

2017: The Transformer

The "Attention Is All You Need" paper introduces the Transformer architecture, revolutionizing natural language processing with its parallel processing capabilities and superior handling of long-range dependencies.

4

2020s: The Rise of LLMs

Large Language Models (LLMs) like those in the Gemini family demonstrate unprecedented capabilities in text generation, summarization, and complex reasoning, bringing conversational AI into the mainstream.

The Engine of Conversation: LLMs

At the heart of modern conversational AI are Large Language Models built on the Transformer architecture. Unlike earlier models focused on narrow tasks, LLMs are trained on vast datasets to develop a general understanding of language, context, and reasoning. This allows them to perform a wide range of tasks without task-specific training.

Anatomy of a Transformer

The Transformer model's key innovation is the attention mechanism, allowing it to weigh the importance of different words in the input text. It consists of two main parts:

Encoder

Reads and processes the entire input sequence (e.g., a user's question) to build a rich contextual representation.

↓ ↑

Decoder

Generates the output sequence (e.g., the AI's answer) word by word, using the encoder's representation and its own previously generated words.

Augmenting Intelligence with RAG

Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) enhances LLMs by grounding them in external, up-to-date information. This process reduces factual inaccuracies and allows the model to draw from proprietary or real-time data sources, making its responses more reliable and relevant.

1. Query: User asks a question.

↓

2. Retrieve: System fetches relevant documents from a knowledge base.

↓

3. Augment: The retrieved context is added to the original prompt.

↓

4. Generate: LLM generates an answer based on both the query and the new context.

The Future: Orchestrated AI Agents

The next evolution is multi-agent systems. Instead of a single monolithic AI, specialized agents collaborate to solve complex problems. An orchestrator agent delegates tasks, manages communication (via protocols like A2A), and synthesizes results, enabling more capable and robust AI applications.
